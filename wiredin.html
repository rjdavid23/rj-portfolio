WiredIn

Helping young professionals stay focused by reducing digital distractions.

WiredIn is a mobile productivity app concept designed as my UX bootcamp capstone project. Inspired by my own experience balancing full-time restaurant work with evening classes, I explored how product design could help people protect their attention and build better focus habits.

The project took me through the complete product design process—from user research and synthesis to interaction design and usability testing—resulting in an interactive Figma prototype validated through two rounds of user testing.

Overview

The Challenge

Staying productive isn't just about managing time—it's about managing attention. While balancing full-time restaurant work with evening UX classes, I experienced how quickly notifications, social media, and other digital distractions could interrupt focus and derail meaningful work. Through interviews with young professionals facing similar challenges, I discovered this wasn't an isolated problem but a common frustration shared by many.

The Solution

WiredIn is a mobile productivity app designed to help users stay focused by minimizing digital distractions while keeping important tasks front and center. Rather than functioning as another to-do list, the app encouraged intentional work sessions through distraction management, task prioritization, and structured focus periods.

My Role

As the sole designer, I led the project from end to end. I conducted user interviews, synthesized research findings, defined product requirements, designed user flows and wireframes, created high-fidelity interfaces, built an interactive Figma prototype, and validated the experience through two rounds of usability testing.

Outcome

The final result was an interactive prototype that successfully addressed the core usability issues uncovered during research and evolved through iterative feedback from users. More importantly, the project taught me how research, testing, and continuous iteration shape better product decisions—lessons that continue to influence my design process today.

Research

The research phase focused on understanding how young professionals move between tools during planning and coordination work. I used interviews, affinity mapping, personas, journey maps, behavioral observations, and workflow analysis to identify recurring points of friction.

"How might we help young professionals limit or prevent interruptions in order to maximize productivity at work?"

System Challenges

  • Fragmented tools made it hard to understand the current state of an event or project
  • Communication overload created repeated clarification loops
  • Lack of visibility made task ownership feel ambiguous
  • Repeated context switching broke focus during already busy workdays
  • Coordination friction increased as more people, tasks, and timelines were added

UX Direction

Instead of treating WiredIn as a simple scheduling product, I framed it as a centralized workflow system. The design direction prioritized clearer ownership, fewer interruptions, better task visibility, and a more predictable structure for event-related work.

Solution

Designed a centralized coordination platform that:

  • Groups event details, tasks, communication, and schedules into one shared workspace
  • Makes ownership, status, and next steps visible without extra follow-ups
  • Reduces reliance on scattered documents, chats, and calendar notes
  • Simplifies event creation, team coordination, scheduling, and task assignment flows

Information Architecture

  • Mapped event creation from initial details to task setup and invite flow
  • Grouped content around overview, schedule, responsibilities, and communication
  • Defined visibility hierarchy so urgent tasks, ownership, and deadlines surfaced first
  • Explored before-and-after workflows to reduce duplicate steps and repeated status checks

Workflow Shift

Before

  • Planning scattered across chat, calendars, notes, and task lists
  • Progress depended on manual updates and repeated reminders
  • Task ownership was hard to scan quickly
  • Interruptions increased whenever context was missing

After

  • Centralized workspace for event coordination
  • Clearer ownership, timelines, and task state
  • Shared visibility into next steps and dependencies
  • Fewer clarification loops during active planning

Testing Takeaways

User testing showed stronger preference for centralized communication, clearer ownership visibility, and simplified task organization. Because the project was conceptual rather than shipped, the impact is framed around validated workflow direction instead of performance metrics.

Highlights

  • Reframed event planning as a workflow visibility and productivity problem
  • Used research synthesis to identify interruption and context-switching patterns
  • Mapped user flows for event creation, team coordination, scheduling, and task assignment
  • Structured the information architecture around ownership, status, and next steps
  • Iterated from low-fidelity wireframes to a tested prototype
