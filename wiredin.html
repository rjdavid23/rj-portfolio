Helping young professionals stay focused by reducing digital distractions.

Overview

The Challenge

Young professionals struggled to stay focused as notifications and digital distractions repeatedly interrupted meaningful work.

The Solution

A mobile productivity app combining distraction management, task prioritization, and structured focus sessions.

My Role

Led the project end-to-end, from research and interaction design to prototyping and usability testing.

Outcome

Validated through two rounds of usability testing, resulting in a simpler and more intuitive prototype.

Why This Problem?

The idea for WiredIn didn't come from a design prompt—it came from my own experience balancing full-time hospitality work with evening UX classes. After speaking with other young professionals, I discovered the same pattern: digital distractions weren't just interrupting productivity—they were making it harder to begin meaningful work in the first place.

Instead of creating another task management app, I wanted to explore how a product could help people become more intentional with their attention and build better focus habits.

Understanding Users

I interviewed 10–15 young professionals to understand how they actually approached productivity—what interrupted their focus and where existing tools fell short. After organizing findings into an affinity map, several consistent themes emerged.

Affinity Map

Key Insights

Knowing what to do wasn't the problem.

Participants knew their responsibilities but struggled to stay focused long enough to complete them.

Small distractions became major interruptions.

Opening a phone with a specific intention often led to unrelated browsing minutes later.

Existing tools felt passive.

Most apps organized tasks but did little to actively reduce distractions during work sessions.

Productivity looked different for everyone.

No single workflow worked for all participants, reinforcing the need for a flexible experience.

The Final Experience

Every feature was designed around a single goal: helping users spend less time managing distractions and more time focusing on meaningful work.

Focus Session
App Blocking

Stay Focused

Users can temporarily block selected applications during dedicated focus sessions. The goal isn't to punish distraction—it's to reduce temptation when maintaining focus matters most.

Task Dashboard

Know What Matters Next

Users rarely struggled to remember their tasks—they struggled to decide where to begin. The dashboard surfaces priorities immediately, reducing hesitation before starting work.

Progress

Build Consistent Habits

Productivity is built through consistency, not single sessions. WiredIn makes focus sessions easy to start, track, and repeat without unnecessary complexity.

Settings

Make It Your Own

Productivity routines vary by person. WiredIn lets users customize blocked apps, focus durations, and task organization to fit their working style.

Validating the Design

I conducted two rounds of usability testing with 5–8 participants each, using an interactive Figma prototype. Their feedback highlighted areas where the experience could be simplified, resulting in several refinements before the final prototype.

Round One — Improving Discoverability

Key actions weren't immediately noticeable. While participants understood the overall flow, several interface elements required unnecessary effort to find.

Changes Made

  • Moved tab controls outside the content frame for better discoverability.
  • Added "Add New" buttons to the Apps, Websites, and Notifications sections.
  • Reduced the Begin Session button size after participants felt it dominated the interface.
Before / After — Round One

Round Two — Simplifying Navigation

The second round focused on reducing visual clutter and minimizing unnecessary interactions throughout the experience.

Changes Made

  • Combined Apps, Websites, and Notifications into a carousel to reduce navigation items.
  • Redesigned the navigation indicator for clearer location feedback.
  • Reduced confirmation modals to let users complete actions with fewer interruptions.

Looking Back

What this project taught me

  • Research should guide decisions—not validate assumptions.
  • Small usability improvements often have the biggest impact.
  • Great products evolve through continuous iteration.

How I'd approach it today

Instead of focusing primarily on blocking distractions, I'd explore AI-assisted planning, intelligent task prioritization, and habit formation to help users decide what deserves their attention before distractions occur.

The technology has changed, but the design principle hasn't: understanding people always comes before designing solutions.
