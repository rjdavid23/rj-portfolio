WiredIn

Helping young professionals stay focused by reducing digital distractions.

WiredIn is a mobile productivity app concept designed as my UX bootcamp capstone project. Inspired by my own experience balancing full-time restaurant work with evening classes, I explored how product design could help people protect their attention and build better focus habits.

The project took me through the complete product design process—from user research and synthesis to interaction design and usability testing—resulting in an interactive Figma prototype validated through two rounds of user testing.

Overview

The Challenge

Staying productive isn't just about managing time—it's about managing attention. While balancing full-time restaurant work with evening UX classes, I experienced how quickly notifications, social media, and other digital distractions could interrupt focus and derail meaningful work. Through interviews with young professionals facing similar challenges, I discovered this wasn't an isolated problem but a common frustration shared by many.

The Solution

WiredIn is a mobile productivity app designed to help users stay focused by minimizing digital distractions while keeping important tasks front and center. Rather than functioning as another to-do list, the app encouraged intentional work sessions through distraction management, task prioritization, and structured focus periods.

My Role

As the sole designer, I led the project from end to end. I conducted user interviews, synthesized research findings, defined product requirements, designed user flows and wireframes, created high-fidelity interfaces, built an interactive Figma prototype, and validated the experience through two rounds of usability testing.

Outcome

The final result was an interactive prototype that successfully addressed the core usability issues uncovered during research and evolved through iterative feedback from users. More importantly, the project taught me how research, testing, and continuous iteration shape better product decisions—lessons that continue to influence my design process today.

Why This Problem?

The idea for WiredIn didn't come from a design prompt—it came from my own routine.

At the time, I was working full-time in hospitality while attending a UX bootcamp in the evenings. After long shifts, finding the motivation to study was already challenging. More often than not, I'd unlock my phone to check a message or quickly browse social media before starting, only to lose track of time without realizing it.

Initially, I assumed it was simply a matter of self-discipline. But after speaking with other young professionals, I discovered many of them experienced the same pattern. Digital distractions weren't just interrupting productivity—they were making it harder to begin meaningful work in the first place.

That realization shifted the direction of the project. Instead of creating another task management app, I wanted to explore how a product could help people become more intentional with their attention and build better focus habits.

Understanding Users

Rather than jumping directly into designing solutions, I wanted to understand how people actually approached productivity in their daily lives. I interviewed 10–15 young professionals with different work and study routines to learn what interrupted their focus, how they managed their tasks, and where existing productivity tools fell short.

After organizing interview findings into an affinity map, several consistent themes began to emerge. While everyone's routines were different, the underlying frustrations were surprisingly similar.

Affinity Map

Key Insights

People didn't struggle with knowing what to do.

Most participants already had a clear understanding of their responsibilities. The challenge wasn't remembering tasks—it was staying focused long enough to complete them.

Small distractions quickly became major interruptions.

Many participants described opening their phones with a specific intention, only to find themselves browsing unrelated apps minutes later. Even short interruptions often made it difficult to return to meaningful work.

Existing productivity apps felt passive.

Most tools focused on organizing tasks but did very little to actively reduce distractions. Users wanted support that helped them stay engaged once they began working.

Productivity looked different for everyone.

No single workflow worked for every participant. This reinforced the importance of creating a flexible experience that users could adapt to their own routines instead of forcing a rigid productivity system.

Persona

Rather than designing for everyone, these insights helped me define a primary audience: young professionals balancing work, study, and personal responsibilities who needed support staying focused—not just staying organized.

From Research to Product Decisions

Research only becomes valuable when it influences design decisions.

Rather than designing features based on assumptions, I used the interview findings to identify opportunities where the product could meaningfully support users. Every core feature in WiredIn was directly inspired by a recurring challenge uncovered during research.

Research Insight

Users lost focus after opening distracting apps.

Design Decision

Help users remove temptation during dedicated work sessions instead of relying solely on self-discipline.

Feature

App Blocking

Users could temporarily block selected applications while a focus session was active, making it easier to stay engaged with the task at hand.

App Blocking Mockup
Research Insight

Users struggled to decide what to work on first.

Design Decision

Surface priorities immediately instead of hiding them behind multiple screens.

Feature

Task Prioritization

Tasks were organized by priority to help users quickly identify what mattered most before starting a focus session.

Task Dashboard
Research Insight

Users became overwhelmed by long task lists.

Design Decision

Encourage users to focus on progress rather than the number of remaining tasks.

Feature

Focus Sessions

Dedicated work sessions helped users commit to one objective at a time while reducing distractions from unfinished work.

Focus Session
Research Insight

Productivity habits varied between users.

Design Decision

Design a flexible experience that could adapt to different routines instead of enforcing a single workflow.

Feature

Customizable Productivity Experience

Users could personalize blocked apps, session durations, and task organization to match their own habits and preferences.

Settings Screen

This approach helped keep the product grounded in user needs rather than assumptions. Every feature existed to solve a specific problem uncovered during research, creating a clearer connection between user insights and the final experience.
