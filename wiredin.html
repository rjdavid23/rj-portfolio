WiredIn

Helping young professionals stay focused by reducing digital distractions.

WiredIn is a mobile productivity app concept designed as my UX bootcamp capstone project. Inspired by my own experience balancing full-time restaurant work with evening classes, I explored how product design could help people protect their attention and build better focus habits.

The project took me through the complete product design process—from user research and synthesis to interaction design and usability testing—resulting in an interactive Figma prototype validated through two rounds of user testing.

Overview

The Challenge

Staying productive isn't just about managing time—it's about managing attention. While balancing full-time restaurant work with evening UX classes, I experienced how quickly notifications, social media, and other digital distractions could interrupt focus and derail meaningful work. Through interviews with young professionals facing similar challenges, I discovered this wasn't an isolated problem but a common frustration shared by many.

The Solution

WiredIn is a mobile productivity app designed to help users stay focused by minimizing digital distractions while keeping important tasks front and center. Rather than functioning as another to-do list, the app encouraged intentional work sessions through distraction management, task prioritization, and structured focus periods.

My Role

As the sole designer, I led the project from end to end. I conducted user interviews, synthesized research findings, defined product requirements, designed user flows and wireframes, created high-fidelity interfaces, built an interactive Figma prototype, and validated the experience through two rounds of usability testing.

Outcome

The final result was an interactive prototype that successfully addressed the core usability issues uncovered during research and evolved through iterative feedback from users. More importantly, the project taught me how research, testing, and continuous iteration shape better product decisions—lessons that continue to influence my design process today.

Why This Problem?

The idea for WiredIn didn't come from a design prompt—it came from my own routine.

At the time, I was working full-time in hospitality while attending a UX bootcamp in the evenings. After long shifts, finding the motivation to study was already challenging. More often than not, I'd unlock my phone to check a message or quickly browse social media before starting, only to lose track of time without realizing it.

Initially, I assumed it was simply a matter of self-discipline. But after speaking with other young professionals, I discovered many of them experienced the same pattern. Digital distractions weren't just interrupting productivity—they were making it harder to begin meaningful work in the first place.

That realization shifted the direction of the project. Instead of creating another task management app, I wanted to explore how a product could help people become more intentional with their attention and build better focus habits.

Understanding Users

Rather than jumping directly into designing solutions, I wanted to understand how people actually approached productivity in their daily lives. I interviewed 10–15 young professionals with different work and study routines to learn what interrupted their focus, how they managed their tasks, and where existing productivity tools fell short.

After organizing interview findings into an affinity map, several consistent themes began to emerge. While everyone's routines were different, the underlying frustrations were surprisingly similar.

Affinity Map

Key Insights

People didn't struggle with knowing what to do.

Most participants already had a clear understanding of their responsibilities. The challenge wasn't remembering tasks—it was staying focused long enough to complete them.

Small distractions quickly became major interruptions.

Many participants described opening their phones with a specific intention, only to find themselves browsing unrelated apps minutes later. Even short interruptions often made it difficult to return to meaningful work.

Existing productivity apps felt passive.

Most tools focused on organizing tasks but did very little to actively reduce distractions. Users wanted support that helped them stay engaged once they began working.

Productivity looked different for everyone.

No single workflow worked for every participant. This reinforced the importance of creating a flexible experience that users could adapt to their own routines instead of forcing a rigid productivity system.

Persona

Rather than designing for everyone, these insights helped me define a primary audience: young professionals balancing work, study, and personal responsibilities who needed support staying focused—not just staying organized.

From Research to Product Decisions

Research only becomes valuable when it influences design decisions.

Rather than designing features based on assumptions, I used the interview findings to identify opportunities where the product could meaningfully support users. Every core feature in WiredIn was directly inspired by a recurring challenge uncovered during research.

Designing the Experience

With the core product direction established, I shifted my focus to designing an experience that felt simple, intentional, and free from unnecessary distractions. Rather than jumping straight into polished interfaces, I explored multiple concepts through user flows, information architecture, and low-fidelity wireframes to validate the overall experience before refining the visual design.

User Flow

Mapping the User Journey

Before designing individual screens, I mapped the user's journey through the product to understand how each interaction connected to the next. This helped identify unnecessary steps, simplify navigation, and ensure the primary actions—adding tasks, starting a focus session, and managing distractions—were always easy to access.

Information Architecture

Organizing the Information

A clear information architecture helped define how content should be grouped and prioritized. Since users often wanted to begin working as quickly as possible, the structure emphasized immediate access to tasks and focus tools while keeping secondary features out of the way.

Wireframes

Exploring Early Concepts

Low-fidelity wireframes allowed me to experiment with layouts and interaction patterns without getting distracted by visual details. This stage made it easier to evaluate different ideas, gather feedback, and iterate quickly before moving into high-fidelity designs.

Iteration Comparison

Iterating Through Feedback

The design evolved continuously throughout the project. Each iteration simplified the experience, improved navigation, and reduced friction based on feedback gathered during testing. By focusing on usability first, I was able to refine the interface with greater confidence before creating the final prototype.

The Final Experience

Every feature in WiredIn was designed to support a single goal: helping users spend less time managing distractions and more time focusing on meaningful work.

Rather than overwhelming users with unnecessary functionality, I prioritized a simple experience that encouraged intentional work habits while remaining flexible enough to fit different routines.

Focus Session
App Blocking

Stay Focused

One of the biggest pain points uncovered during research was how easily users became distracted after unlocking their phones. To address this, WiredIn allowed users to temporarily block selected applications during dedicated focus sessions.

The goal wasn't to punish users for checking their phones—it was to reduce temptation at moments when maintaining focus mattered most.

Task Dashboard

Know What Matters Next

Research showed that users rarely struggled to remember their tasks. Instead, they struggled to decide where to begin.

The task dashboard surfaced priorities immediately, helping users start meaningful work with less hesitation and fewer decisions.

Progress

Build Consistent Habits

Productivity isn't achieved through one successful work session—it's built through consistency over time.

WiredIn encouraged users to establish routines by making focus sessions easy to start, track, and repeat without adding unnecessary complexity.

Settings

Make It Your Own

Everyone approaches productivity differently.

Rather than enforcing a rigid workflow, WiredIn allowed users to customize blocked applications, focus durations, and task organization so the experience could adapt to different habits and working styles.

The final prototype brought together the research, design decisions, and usability findings into a cohesive experience that prioritized focus over feature quantity. Every screen was designed with the same objective: helping users spend less time fighting distractions and more time accomplishing meaningful work.

Validating the Design

Design doesn't end when the interface looks polished. To evaluate whether WiredIn actually helped users stay focused, I conducted two rounds of usability testing using an interactive Figma prototype.

Each round included 5–8 participants completing realistic tasks while thinking aloud as they navigated the product. Their feedback highlighted areas where the experience could be simplified, resulting in several refinements before the final prototype.

Round One — Improving Discoverability

The first round of testing revealed that some key actions weren't immediately noticeable. While participants understood the overall flow, several interface elements required unnecessary effort to find.

Changes Made

  • Moved the controls for switching between the Apps and Websites tabs outside the content frame, making them easier to discover.
  • Added an "Add New" button to the Apps, Websites, and Notifications sections, giving users a clearer starting point when creating blocklists.
  • Reduced the size of the Begin Session button after participants felt it dominated the interface and drew too much attention.
Before / After — Round One

Round Two — Simplifying Navigation

The second round focused on streamlining the experience by reducing visual clutter and minimizing unnecessary interactions.

Changes Made

  • Combined the Apps, Websites, and Notifications sections into a carousel within the Blocklist feature, reducing the number of navigation items and making the interface easier to scan.
  • Redesigned the navigation indicator to provide clearer feedback about the user's current location within the app.
  • Reduced the number of confirmation modals throughout the experience, allowing users to complete common actions with fewer interruptions.
Before / After — Round Two

Key Takeaway

The biggest lesson from usability testing wasn't that users struggled to understand the product—it was that small interface refinements had a meaningful impact on the overall experience. Improving discoverability, simplifying navigation, and reducing unnecessary interactions made the product feel more intuitive without changing its core functionality.
